Your postcode decides your fate before you make a choice.
Life expectancy varies by 7 years across Malaysian states.
This is not natural variation — it is a policy fingerprint. A map shows where the problem is. To understand why, we must follow the money.

Chapter 2 — Where the Doctors Are Not

SCENE 2 / 10

"Out of sight, out of mind."

3×

KL has 37.4 doctors per 10,000. Sabah has 12.3. The map makes the gap visible.

72%

Of Malaysians live below the national average. East Malaysia has 13.9 doctors per 10,000 for 5.9 million people.

Malaysia has enough doctors — on paper.
The problem: they are all in three cities.
A national ratio hides a map of near-total absence.
Sabah and Sarawak share 8,300 doctors for 5.9 million people. Distance from a doctor is not just a statistic. Some states have been more systematically abandoned. Let's name them.

Chapter 3 — The Two Malaysias

SCENE 3 / 10

"Which side of the fence are you on?"

4 states

Sabah, Terengganu, Pahang, and Labuan rank Critical — lowest doctor supply, highest child mortality.

4 tiers

The divide is categorical. Four tiers reveal a structural separation, not a spectrum.

Continuous gradients make inequality look comfortable — a spectrum.
The truth is harsher: states fall into discrete tiers.
Critical, Under-resourced, Adequate, Well-served — the divide is structural.
Sabah, Terengganu, and Pahang land at the bottom.
KL, Penang, and Perlis sit comfortably at the top. These tiers did not appear overnight. The same map has looked this way for two decades.

Chapter 4 — Stuck in the Same Place

SCENE 4 / 10

"The more things change, the more they stay the same."

+1.1%

Terengganu's infant mortality rate in 2023 is almost identical to 2000. Twenty-three years. Virtually no progress.

3 states

Sabah, Pahang, and Terengganu all remain above 9.5 per 1,000 in 2023 — anchored in the Critical tier.

Strip away national averages and progress is deeply uneven.
Every row fades lighter — overall improvement is real.
But Sabah, Pahang, and Terengganu remain stubbornly dark.
KL and Penang were always light.
Sabah's early improvement was better registration — not better outcomes. The same states keep appearing in the dark columns. Geography determines who can afford to get sick.

Chapter 5 — Who Pays for Getting Sick

SCENE 5 / 10

"One bad hand can clean out the whole table."

37%

Nearly double the WHO's 20% benchmark. Patients bear the heaviest share.

2×

Malaysia's out-of-pocket share is double the WHO's 20% safety benchmark. The system rations care by income.

Every 100 ringgit: 51 from government, 37 from pockets, 12 from insurance.
The red squares fill more than a third of the grid.
One hospitalisation — RM 5,000 to 15,000 — can erase months of wages.
The sicker you are, the poorer you get. Money protects the rich from getting sicker. But it cannot protect Malaysia's children.

Chapter 6 — The Infrastructure of Illness

SCENE 6 / 10

"Rot starts from the roots."

21.2%

Almost matching Cambodia — a country with one-fifth of Malaysia's income.

11.4 pp

Every comparable-income country achieves below 8% stunting. Malaysia does not.

At Malaysia's income level, stunting should be under 10%.
It is 21%. Inside Malaysia, Sabah reaches 35%.
That rate is typical of Sub-Saharan Africa.
This starts before a child ever sees a hospital.
It starts with clean water, food, and a safe home. A malnourished child becomes a sick adult. Treating a lifetime of preventable illness has a price tag — and it has been rising every year.

Chapter 7 — The Rising Tide of Cost

SCENE 7 / 10

"A rising tide lifts all boats — unless yours has a hole in it."

3.7×

OOP spending grew 3.7× since 2000. The red band outpaced all other funding sources.

2016

After 2016, private healthcare inflation outpaced income growth. Patients have paid more every year since.

Malaysia's healthcare bill has grown every decade.
But the rising tide does not lift all boats equally.
Out-of-pocket costs have surged since 2016.
Government spending grew — but private inflation outran it.
Without savings or insurance, there is no safety net. The costs are rising. But what is actually killing Malaysians — and has the picture changed?

Chapter 8 — What Is Killing Us

SCENE 8 / 10

"Know your enemy."

76%

NCDs — diabetes, heart disease, cancer — are preventable with early care. Without it, catastrophically expensive.

16%

Communicable diseases are receding nationally — but in Critical-tier states, the burden remains far higher.

In one generation, what kills Malaysians has completely shifted.
Communicable diseases have receded to just 16% nationally.
NCDs now claim 76%: diabetes, heart disease, stroke, cancer.
These are preventable — with early screening and regular care.
The B40 are the ones who miss those screenings. We know what is killing us. We know where it is worst. Is the gap still widening?

Chapter 9 — Progress, or the Illusion of It?

SCENE 9 / 10

"Two steps forward, one step back."

−36%

Kelantan fell from 13.5 to 8.6 per 1,000 — the sharpest improvement in Malaysia.

10.6

The highest rate in Malaysia. The same state that appears in every chapter.

Malaysia's national mortality trend looks like a success story.
Unpack it by state and the picture changes.
Kelantan fell from 13.5 to 8.6 — the sharpest drop nationally.
Sabah's 2014 jump was a registration reform, not a crisis.
The gap at 2023 remains significant. Progress is real — but it has a postcode. Are we improving fast enough?

Chapter 10 — Falling Behind in the Region?

SCENE 10 / 10

"In the land of the blind, the one-eyed man is king — until he isn't."

#2

Up from #4 in 2000. The headline story is improvement.

Thailand

From #5 to #2 in 20 years, starting lower. A warning, not a comfort.

Malaysia once ranked 4th in ASEAN life expectancy.
By 2022, it sits at 2nd — a genuine improvement.
But Thailand rose from 5th to 2nd in 20 years.
Others are moving faster, starting from lower bases.
Malaysia's internal inequality is the anchor holding it back.
"We keep treating the symptom and ignoring the disease.
The postcode a Malaysian baby is born into shouldn't determine how long they live.
But right now, in 2024, it does.
And until we're honest about why — the map won't change."
